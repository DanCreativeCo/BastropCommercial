Commercial and development land is a fundamentally different sale than
residential. The buyer pool is smaller, more sophisticated, and underwriting the
*future* of the dirt — not its current state. Pricing and positioning have to
speak to that.

## Value lives in optionality

A parcel's worth is driven by what it *can become*: zoning, utility access,
frontage, topography, and timing relative to the growth curve. The seller's job
— really, the broker's job — is to surface and document that optionality so a
buyer can underwrite it with confidence.

## Reaching qualified buyers

Most commercial land never reaches the right buyer because it's marketed like a
generic listing. The deals that close are the ones presented with a real
diligence package, professional aerials and survey detail, and direct outreach
to the developers and investors actually active in the submarket.
